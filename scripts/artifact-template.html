Meeting overlay

Five minutes before a meeting, a pixel detective walks in along the bottom of the screen. He stops, a speech bubble snaps open with the meeting name and time, he raises his magnifying glass at it, and after ten seconds he walks back out. The window is transparent, always on top, and click-through — it floats over whatever you're doing and never blocks the mouse.

Below you can change the meeting, how long he holds, how fast he walks, and his size. The desktop app reads your real calendar from one iCal URL; the copy button gives you the full build prompt for your own setup.

Fluid springs

    
      
    
  


  


  

    

      
Apple's fluid-interface motion, rebuilt with no animation library. The card above is a real physical object: grab it — even mid-flight — and it tracks your cursor 1:1 from the exact point you grabbed. Flick it and the spring inherits your release velocity, projects the momentum forward, and lands on the corner the flick was heading to. Drag past the edges and it rubber-bands instead of stopping.

      
The recipes come from the apple-design skill — WWDC's "Designing Fluid Interfaces," translated to pointer events and requestAnimationFrame. Below you can tune the two parameters Apple designs with: damping (overshoot) and response (speed), plus the deceleration rate that decides how far a flick throws.

The sheet

    
      
    
  


  


  

    

      
The interaction everyone knows from iOS, rebuilt from the apple-design skill's drawer recipe. Drag the sheet and it tracks your cursor 1:1; release, and the speed of your gesture decides what happens — a fast downward flick dismisses it even from near the top, while a slow drag to the same spot springs back to the nearest resting point. As the sheet rises, the content behind it dims and pushes back, all derived from one position so the three layers move as a single object. Past the top it rubber-bands.

      
Below you can tune Apple's shipped drawer values — damping 0.8, response 0.3s — and how hard the background dims. Grab the sheet mid-flight anytime; it never locks you out.

Frosted materials

    
      
    
  


  


  

    

      
The minimal register of the materials chapter: one control, cropped close, on a surface that's almost nothing — a gray ground, a hairline divider, the white corner of a card. All the design lives in the pill: a sky-blue gradient that reads curved because of two soft white blooms, a bright rim ring where the glass edge catches light, and a sheen that follows your pointer. Press it and it gives; drag it anywhere and it springs back to center at the speed you threw it.

      
Below you can retune the three qualities that define the look — how vivid the blue is, how hard the edge catches light, and how strong the hover sheen glows. Everything else is restraint.

Micro buttons

    
      
    
  


  


  

    

      
The nine ways a button can talk back, one pill each: an arrow that slides in to say "this goes somewhere," a star that bursts, a label that morphs through busy into done, a copy that confirms itself, a heart that beats, a gear that turns, a delete that shakes and asks "Sure?", a bell that rings, and a sun that becomes a moon. Every one is plain CSS and a few lines of state — no animation library.

      
The taxonomy comes from Amicro, Syed Subhan's catalogue of micro-transitions, rebuilt from scratch in the vault's light language. Two variables drive the whole set's feel — try the sliders: Speed divides every duration at once, and Pop sets how far the click overshoots. Left alone, the set demos itself.
